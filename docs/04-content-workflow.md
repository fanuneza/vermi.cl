# vermi.cl Content Workflow

## Purpose

This document defines the complete editorial workflow for producing content for vermi.cl.

Its goal is consistency, scientific rigor, and long-term quality.

Every article should follow this workflow.

Never skip steps.

---

# Guiding principle

The objective is **not** to write quickly.

The objective is to publish content that remains one of the best available resources in Spanish for many years.

Take the time necessary to think before writing.

---

# Editorial process

Every article should be developed in the following order.

1. Understand the search intent.
2. Define the audience.
3. Research the evidence.
4. Build an outline.
5. Identify canonical entities.
6. Write the first draft.
7. Verify every factual claim.
8. Improve readability.
9. Optimize for retrieval.
10. Perform a final editorial review.

Only after completing all steps should the article be considered finished.

---

# Step 1: Understand the search intent

Determine what the reader is actually trying to accomplish.

Questions to answer:

- What problem are they experiencing?
- What information do they already know?
- What misconception are they likely to have?
- What outcome are they hoping for?

Never optimize for keywords alone.

Optimize for solving the reader's problem.

---

# Step 2: Define the audience

Choose one primary audience.

- Beginner
- Intermediate
- Advanced

Secondary audiences are acceptable, but never write for everyone simultaneously.

The article should primarily satisfy one reader profile.

---

# Step 3: Research

Research should prioritize evidence in this order.

## Tier 1

Peer-reviewed scientific papers.

## Tier 2

University extension publications.

## Tier 3

Government publications.

## Tier 4

Books written by recognized experts.

## Tier 5

Experienced practitioners.

Avoid relying on blogs unless they provide original evidence.

---

# Research synthesis

Never summarize papers independently.

Instead ask:

What does the overall evidence suggest?

Where do studies agree?

Where do they disagree?

Which recommendations are most strongly supported?

The final article should represent the consensus of available evidence whenever possible.

---

# Step 4: Build the outline

Before writing a single paragraph, define:

- H1
- Introduction
- H2 sections
- H3 subsections
- tables
- diagrams
- FAQs
- internal links
- external references

Every heading should have a clear purpose.

---

# Step 5: Identify canonical entities

List all important concepts.

Examples:

- Eisenia fetida
- Vermicompost
- Humus
- Lixiviado
- Carbono
- Nitrógeno
- Humedad
- Temperatura
- Microorganismos

Whenever one already has a canonical article on vermi.cl, avoid redefining it unnecessarily.

Instead, summarize briefly and link to the canonical page.

---

# Step 6: Write the first draft

The first draft should prioritize completeness.

Do not edit while writing.

Focus on answering every important question.

Every recommendation should explain:

- what
- why
- how
- prevention

---

# Step 7: Verify factual claims

After drafting, review every factual statement.

For each claim ask:

Can this be supported?

Is this universally true?

Is there uncertainty?

Would a scientist object to this wording?

If confidence is low, rewrite.

---

# Step 8: Improve readability

Review the article specifically for readability.

Remove:

- repetition
- filler
- unnecessary introductions
- vague language
- passive constructions when unnecessary

Shorten paragraphs.

Improve transitions.

Clarify explanations.

---

# Step 9: Optimize for retrieval

The article should be easy for both humans and language models to understand.

Include:

- concise definitions
- comparison tables
- bullet lists
- numbered procedures
- troubleshooting tables
- FAQs
- summaries

Avoid hidden assumptions.

Define terms before using them extensively.

---

# Step 10: Editorial review

Perform a final review using the checklist below.

---

# Scientific review checklist

Verify that:

- every recommendation has evidence
- no numbers are invented
- uncertainty is acknowledged
- biological explanations are correct
- terminology is consistent
- no myths are repeated as facts

---

# Editorial review checklist

Verify that:

- the introduction answers the question
- headings are descriptive
- explanations are logical
- every recommendation explains why
- the conclusion summarizes the key points

---

# Chile review checklist

Verify that:

- seasons are correct
- climate advice is localized
- examples are familiar
- measurements use SI units
- suppliers are current
- municipal information has been verified

---

# SEO review checklist

Verify that:

- search intent is fully satisfied
- title accurately reflects the content
- headings follow a logical hierarchy
- internal links are identified
- related articles are suggested naturally

Do not add keywords unnaturally.

---

# LLM review checklist

Ask the following.

If another language model retrieved only this page, would it understand:

- the definitions?
- the conclusions?
- the recommendations?
- the evidence?
- the limitations?

If not, improve clarity.

---

# Required article components

Unless intentionally omitted, every article should contain:

- concise answer
- explanation
- step-by-step instructions
- common mistakes
- troubleshooting
- prevention
- FAQ
- summary
- internal links
- references

---

# Frequently asked questions

Before publication, identify at least five realistic follow-up questions a reader might ask.

Answer them.

This naturally improves completeness and search coverage.

---

# Tables

Prefer tables whenever they improve understanding.

Examples:

- food comparisons
- temperature ranges
- moisture indicators
- troubleshooting
- myths versus facts
- safe versus unsafe inputs
- seasonal adjustments

Tables should simplify decisions.

---

# Visual opportunities

For every article, identify whether one or more of the following would improve learning.

- annotated diagrams
- flowcharts
- lifecycle illustrations
- comparison graphics
- decision trees
- timelines
- process diagrams

Educational visuals are preferred over decorative illustrations.

---

# Internal linking strategy

Every article should identify opportunities to link to:

- prerequisite concepts
- related problems
- advanced reading
- canonical entity pages

Links should help readers learn, not simply improve SEO.

---

# Future updates

When revisiting an article:

Do not simply append new information.

Instead:

- verify scientific accuracy
- update references
- improve explanations
- simplify wording
- expand sections that readers commonly misunderstand

Every revision should make the article objectively better.

---

# Definition of done

An article is complete only when all of the following are true.

The primary question is answered immediately.

The biological reasoning is explained.

The practical advice is actionable.

Scientific claims have been verified.

The article is localized for Chile.

The writing is clear and concise.

Canonical concepts are linked.

The article would still be useful five years from today.

An experienced editor would struggle to identify meaningful improvements.
